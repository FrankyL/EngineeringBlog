Massive 3D-Printed 7-Segment LED Clock

Franky Laurentis June 30, 2025

Pretty ancient build. Built this like back in 2023, 1st unit is still in SG right now as of 2025, 2nd unit is in Indonesia at my dad's business (LOL apparently the damn thing is so bright it can been seen across the street from the shophouse at night)

1st unit is truly a trial run of an idea, only show hours and minutes while 2nd unit has seconds also.

Pic above is unit 1 that's in SG, you can see the trapezoidal connection points to the right and left of each digit

Pic above is the back view, basically just 3 wires coming out of the each digit and connecting to the next one. Look at the rectangle patches inside the build, those are LED strips that I cut and then manually connected with solder.

Unit 2 at my dad's shop back home ("depot air minum" (water filtration shop) in Indonesian, might be a really strange concept for S'poreans), look at the seconds, 2 more digits compared to unit 1. Also I have a criminal lack of photos of this unit, I had to dig through my chatlogs to find this photo. Good thing I made this thing modular to begin with, quite easy to stuff the modules into my dad's baggage and just haul it back home and then reassembling by himself.

The 3D printing is the least of my problem, the soldering afterwards is VERY time consuming if I don't plan ahead. I made them digit by digit and then just connect the wires together, i.e. the 1st hour digit goes to the left of 2nd hour digit and so on and so forth. This is possible cuz I used the WS2812B LED strip with their own individually programmable LEDs and if you have used them before, you will also know this LED have input and output + VCC and GND. The idea is to chain these RGB LEDs together and you can address them one by one in a line.

Here's a sample of WS2812 LED strip, you can see the DO and DI

Used light diffuser plates on each segment of the digits for the first time ever and it's a very interesting material. Only problem is the gluing onto the print, I used hot glue and it might fall off after some time, but this one holds up 2 years later so I guess it is better than originally expected.

Difference in code

Unit 2 is an improvement on unit 1. Unit 1 connects to Wi-Fi to get the local time, then update accordingly. It will do a check every hour to verify that it's still the correct time.

Unit 2 has seconds and I added an offline version (well, I call it offline cuz the original requires Wi-Fi, thus that one is online). More complicated cuz I had to simulate an actual clock + occasional check if it can connect to the designated Wi-Fi SSID.

IIRC I even added buttons and switches to do the whole offline value up-down thing (like an actual clock with the dial) while displaying the numbers and everything. All around troublesome and hilariously, in the end, my dad ended up using the original Wi-Fi mode anyway cuz that one sets up everything automatically. Oh well, good practice.

Conclusion

Overall, I think it's a promising product to sell but the time it takes to just make 1 is like 1 week minimum and there's lots of soldering to do. The fastest way is to perhaps make PCBs for each segment and then using just headers or something to connect the different PCBs together.

Oh wait hang on jumper wires might just work, easy solution, no crimping no soldering, just make sure it sticks around with hot glue and the size of the build will definitely be able to accommodate jumper wires.
