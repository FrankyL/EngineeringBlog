Introduction

Me love tank treads, tank treads are love, tank treads are life.

Jokes aside, tank treads are a huge pain in the ass to make, 3D printing is one thing, making them (i.e. joining them all together into a tread) is another.

Do note that this project is old, I think at least 2 years old from the day this post is written. I wouldn't be the least bit surprised if the PLA has started to crumble.

IIRC back then, I used this "gardening steel wire" that I got from a hardware store across the street. It got the job done but my hand is aching by the time I'm done.

I circled the gardening wires I used between the treads, basically ram em in, bend to 90 degrees, then use a really huge plier to cut them, hoping the bent wire will keep itself there naturally

it's a pretty hacky job but works decently enough

Wah I forgot where's the Arduino Nano for this. This is when you know the project is ancient, I'm still using Arduino Nano + NRF24L01. These days I just use ESP32 and wing the controls with Wi-Fi. I don't remember where I misplaced the NRF24 controller too, dang it all past me, why would you do this?

Remaking the tank treads but now in PETG instead of PLA

PETG should last longer than PLA, surprisingly OK material. Higher temperature needed at 240/80C. Bought them cuz ChatGPT suggested it might be better for 3D printing custom guitar necks (really long story)

Some images of the tank treads how I connected them with one another before I embark on this maddening journey of wire bending. If you're wondering, I used steel wire as "connector" instead of 3D printing it is cuz 3D printed pegs sucks, especially at that size (2-3mm max is weak af, especially if it need to withstand vibration and movements)

Steps are like:

  1. Clean the print up, slice out the supports (kinda regretted using the brim one, forgot to change it prior to print)

  2. Eyeball how much garden steel wire you need (Got the 0.9mm one from Daiso, works pretty well, I think the previous one uses 1.2mm cuz I remembered needing to use a vice drill to enlarge the hole a bit

  3. Cut out a bunch of small ~1.5cm wire pieces, bend one end to around 90 degrees (just roughly is fine)

  4. Insert the straight end to the max, use plier to bend the other end in the same direction as where you bent in step 3 (VERY IMPORTANT)

  5. Rotate the wire so that its perpendicular to the tank tread (Can be pokey and sharp cuz steel wire)

  6. Use plier to squeeze the 2 bent sides against the print (Don't need too hard, just enough to make it hold nicely)

  7. Repeat until you get 2 tank treads. (For me, each side has 30 pieces, 30x2 = 60 pieces in total. 120 wires in total needed :'^| )

Honestly, hard to convey how to do it, but as long as you get the rough idea and what each step is trying to do is fine, adapt it to the tools you have at hand.

Sacrificed range and speed compared to NRF24L01, comparing the 3 methods and why I bothered to make something like this: (Different for ESP32-S3 and newer ones probably, I'm talking about the cheapest & classic WROOM)

[BONUS 2] Old Arduino Mega Based NRF24L01 Remote Controller

This is the front view of the NRF24L01 Controller that I refurbished a couple of months back, Arduino Mega is big chungus holy moly. The PCB can accommodate 2 mini OLEDs (I2C type), 6 buttons, 6 trimmers and 4 joystick modules.

Why so many? Just for fun haha, also I compressed the overall range of the joystick (resolution 1024 for Arduinos, 4096 for ESP32) into 1 byte (256).

Why compression? I realized halfway thru typing out "100" occupies 3 whole bytes already, and the transmission sometimes gets garbled, so better to compress if you don't need the resolution.

In addition, I think even 4 bits is enough cuz these joysticks are really not that precise and you end up maxxing it right away.

This is the back view of the same NRF24L01 Controller. The casing is new and I crammed as many batteries as I can, I think Arduino's regulator can withstand above 12V? This one is 8*1.5V, just nice 12V too.

The wiring is a bit messy and is also cuz this is not "casing" in the strictest sense, just a 3D printed frame to hold the electronics.
