To exchange or not? (NUS-TITech Exchange SEP ACAP)

Franky Laurentis June 8, 2025 ACAP

Sike! You think the last post is the end of my NUS stuff? I think not!

Just joking :^) , this is more B.Eng. EE than the M.Sc. ME too actually. I just thought better to write stuff down before I forgot even more stuff as time passes. This is more or less regarding my exchange to TITech in Tokyo back in 2018. (Holy moly, that's a whopping 7 years ago). Also TITech has not been a thing since 2022 apparently, it's the Institute of Science Tokyo now...Well, technically still "NUS Module Review and Reflection", so Imma just categorize it the same as usual!

IIRC back then the programme is called ACAP (Academic Course Access Program).

Some special pointers cuz I have got colleagues (after graduation of course) asking me about it:

  1. School fee still same as NUS, which is actually more expensive than the TITech one, but that's a can of worms I am not going to open today. Might actually be a good thing if you decide to exchange to the US or UK which somehow has a way more expensive tuition fees than NUS.

  2. Need to find a prof. to take you in, back then I managed to get Prof. Masayuki Fujita (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masayuki_Fujita) to do so. Many thanks to him, and I end up getting quite a lot of exposure to a robotics lab and applying Unity Vive VR (Yes, ancient tech by now, I know.) as a controller in a robotics context.

  3. Found 2 Indonesians and 1 Malaysian PhD. dudes (1 of them is from Bali, quite fascinating ngl) in the lab, so hilariously enough, I can actually get by with only English and Indonesian doing activities in the lab.

  4. JP unis are split into 4 quarters iirc? I remember taking like 2 quarters worth of stuff, though those 2 quarters is actually equivalent to 1 sem in SG

  5. NUS EE admins don't give a shit LMAO, we just whacked 1 Masters Controls Mod (Robust Control, also masters cuz bachelors mods over there taught in japanese) and somehow matched it to a year 3 Controls Mod. Apparently CS admins is way more strict about this, we found a CS guy who can hardly map anything back.

  6. VISA is a huge pain in the ass. Had to get a letter of recommendation and letter of eligibility and then haul ass to the JP embassy at Orchard, then back and forth to make sure everything matches up. Honestly, if you are Indonesian/SEA too and is worried about visa, as long as your uni is legit or you are in NUS, it should be fine imo. I think me being in NUS and I already have a letter of support helps a lot and smoothed a lot of things out. Also the procedure for my S'porean friend is the same, cuz he is also applying for a student visa (default tourist 3-months visa free thing is kinda useless here), so he has to jump through the same hoops as me.

  7. My dorm is the Aobadai one, the suburb one, technically part of Yokohama area one. SGD 200 after conversion a month, pretty cheap but 1 hour away from the campus at Ookayama, yes 2 Os, not 1. I know a friend who got assigned to the dormitory nearer to Tokyo, near Meguro, but it costs like SGD600 and smaller in size, so really have trade-offs.

  8. Their "Supermarket" has less stuff than Donki Hote (Don Quijote, yes, Don Don Donki in SG), and I can't find choco milk or Milo. Also good idea to learn how to cook, though this is easy cuz they have lots of frozen stuff that is very delicious just by microwaving. Sushi is generally cheaper, but don't make my mistake of buying the 1000 yen pack, then remembering they would suck if you put in fridge overnight and have to finish them on the same night.

  9. Don't trust the signs in their stations, especially Shibuya. In SG, you can follow the signs to get to where u want, in Shibuya, they will get you lost. Follow the Google Maps direction instead, they are better.

  10. When taking strolls, don't assume their streets will be as well planned as SG's. I have encountered dead-ends out of nowhere or the street don't connect back when you do the turn-right-4-times. Make sure to bring power bank if your phone is running low so that you can Google Maps back from whichever boonies you wander into. (Still safer than the boonies back home (there's boars and tapirs), so don't be too worried, though seeing mini shrines beside the street at 1AM will definitely creep anyone out, give your prayers tho if you want some fortune buff.)

  11. Spring might be cold (around April/May IIRC), I was chattering my ass off after the shower, just something to note if you rarely goes outside of the equator (like me)

  12. Hanami(花見) or flower-viewing is overrated NGL. It is nice in the first 10 mins, but the whole drinking sesh lasted 2 hours. By the end of it my ass is sore sitting on the floor and buzzed from the drinks. Solid 5/10 experience, try it once if you want to know what's the hype.

  13. Maid cafe is omega-cringe. Tried it once with 2 friends, don't really get the point and the 2nd hand embarrassment is very intense haha. Still a very memorable experience but I will never want to do it again.

Looks like it's still around and kicking, I remember Negishi-san in the admin and the cleaners are all very nice and patient. My kana handwriting is kinda bad and took me forever to write フランキー.ローレンティス(lit. Furankii Roorentis), took quite a bit for her to figure out what I have been writing haha.

I think this dorm experience is what caused me to have a particular soft spot for quiet suburbs even though for all concerns and purposes I live in a small industrial town (ID) (Jakarta people in SG regarded me as kind of an "Orang Kampungan" (village person)) and huge city (SG) (My hometown people regarded me as "Anak Kota" (City kid)). The place is chill and quiet and a bit on a hill. I remember taking a stroll towards Tana station next door and being able to see Mount Fuji and the Mountain ranges behind and beside it

Not my photo but similar to what I saw, just found out that Aoba-ku is kind of well known for the hidden view of the mountain ranges, it's beautiful. Highly recommend exploring stuff like this one on your own, not everything need to follow tour guides, if you know what I mean

Yokohama is great fun, underrated compared to Tokyo

Also participated in Comiket and Wonderfest for the first time and haven't got the time to do it again, prolly not any time soon now that I have more big-ticket stuff to pay off.

My grades during the exchange and the mods I took, my impression is this: In NUS, easy to go in (for S'poreans, still tough for foreigners lol), hard to grad. In JP, hard to go in, easy to grad. There's lots of people in engineering playing around or enjoying their youth (リア充めw), that's something unimaginable in NUS, where most of us are stressed on the daily.

The lab work is not that heavy overall (your mileage may vary), I just need to figure out how to use Unity Vive VR headsets they recently acquired and inform them how to use it. I end up making a small app on Unity that controls small indicator in VR which is basically a waypoint for their robot swarm to gather around. Something like that?

Something to note: Lab work activities can be really different to everyone, I know 4 samples, me included:

  1. Me: 2-3 days in lab per week, I can run off the Akiba or Yokohama once in a while

  2. NUS EE S'porean guy who went on this SEP with me: 0 lab activities, he has fun the whole time. Can't say I'm not jelly af but such is life.

  3. NUS CS S'porean guy we met over there: unknown, he participated in like 5-6 CCAs (Clubs) in TITech, the absolute madman, so I assume his lab probably about the same as mine.

  4. NTU ME S'porean guy I met after graduation: Everyday in lab, also doing robotics like me, didn't really get to run around during his exchange.

My overall opinion about Exchange to Japan: If you have not hopped out of your country before, do it. 6 months in another country is totally different from going on a tour and leaving in 2 weeks time. It exposes you to a new experience and a different way of life and I know 1 S'porean guy who made up his mind to make permanently migrating to JP his end-game.

Finally, y'all must try Abura soba 油そば, good stuff. Don't overdo it though, definitely not good for your cardiovascular health. There's a really good one at Akiba called Kasugatei.

And if you're looking for tsukemen, there's a really good one right next to TITech Ookayama, Ryoga ramen. Don't mind the 3.5 stars, Japanese people ain't ever gonna give 5 stars, 4 stars is their "perfect".

There used to be a Tora no Ana(とらのあな) at Machida (町田), the nearest city to my dorm back in the day, but they closed in the aftermath of COVID around 2020. End of an era, but then again also expected with the rise of online shops. Fortunately BOOKOFF (Basically a more general version of Surugaya (駿河屋), Their Cash Converter, 2nd hand shop i.e. basically new stuff but cheaper cuz Japanese people are really good at keeping their possessions) and Tsutaya (CD/Game rentals) still there when I checked recently.
